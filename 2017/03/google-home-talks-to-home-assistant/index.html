Google Home talks to Home Assistant... (Sort of!)

March 15, 2017 in Home Automation · 4 minute read

I had been waiting to get my hands on Google Home since it was first announced, and was trying to find all the valid reasons not to jump on the Amazon Echo bandwagon yet.

My reasons
  1. It’s not Google
  2. From what I’ve read and seen in the reviews, Alexa is not as sophisticated as Google Assistant
  3. Most of Amazon’s services are not available in Australia yet
  4. And lastly, it’s NOT Google

And then they released it only in the US. All dreams crushed!!! But hey, Echo is not available in Australia either. And it may take even longer to get released here. So I should wait? Nah, I am getting it from the USofA. And finally after a while, thanks to my sister-in-law who lives there, I’ve got one to play with now. The fiddling begins…

Linking it to Home Assistant

First thing I wanted to try was to hook it up into my home automation setup. Home Assistant is currently my central piece of software that drives all my IoT devices in the setup. But as of yet Google Home could only control a handful of devices like SmartThings, Nest, Philips Hue, etc. Home Assistant was not one of them. Luckily, the smart guys and contributors at Home Assistant had this figured out. There is a ‘Emulated Hue Bridge’ component that basically provides a virtual Philips Hue bridge that Google Home could talk to. What this means is, this component fakes devices and controls in Home Assistant into a JSON feed that Google Home can consume and control via Hue API. Google Home sees all of them as hue lights that it can turn on and off or change the brightness of.

The Configuration

To enable and configure Emulated Hue Bridge for Google Home, this is the minimum configuration that you will need to add to your configuration.yaml file. Make sure that listen_port is set to 80 as Google Home does not work with other ports.

emulated_hue:
  type: google_home  
  listen_port: 80

The following is my configuration where I have limited the control to certain domains. The exposed_domains variable allows you to specify what domain entities are visible for Google Home to control. For majority of you and your configuration, you would want to limit the domains entities. You wouldn’t want everything to be controlled as lights, as that’s how Google Home sees them.

emulated_hue:
  type: google_home
  host_ip: 192.168.1.8
  listen_port: 80
  expose_by_default: true
  exposed_domains:
    - light
    - switch
    - input_boolean
    - media_player

Check out the full configuration guide on Emulated Hue Bridge to learn more on the variables and the possibilities. If you are new to Home Assistant, start here first.

Once the configuration is loaded, you can verify that it is working correctly (and the world hasn’t fallen apart) by hitting these two urls:

http://<HA IP Address>/description.xml - returns descriptor file in XML format
http://<HA IP Address>/api/pi/lights - returns the above feed, list of entities in JSON

The Link

Now go to the Google Home app in your phone and Home Control > Devices > Add devices to add this as Philips Hue. It should pull through the devices in the feed. To make it easier to control them, assign them to rooms.

Android Google Home app

Enjoy the luxury

Now command your assistant like a king!

And once in a while, make sure to get up and exercise those thumgs and fingers.

Hey, Google... turn on all lights may not be a desirable command here as everything is a light to Google Home. It will turn on your media server, or open your garage door if you have one, which will be annoying. The workaround is to assign your lights to "Rooms" in Google Home app and invoke the commands by room. Hey, Google... turn on all lights in the living room. You can also create a room called "House", assign all lights to it and say Hey, Google... turn on all lights in the house.
